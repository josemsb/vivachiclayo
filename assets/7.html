The “Valley of the Pyramids”
Túcume is a historical town that was occupied by the Sicán culture around 1100 AD after the Lambayeque people abandoned their land in the “Bosque de Pomac”. Their time in Túcume marked the peak of their civilization, as evidenced by their master metalwork and accomplished seamanship. Today, Túcume is known as the “Valley of the Pyramids”, as it is the home of 26 adobe pyramids scattered across the land. Besides these majestic tombs rising above the treeline, there are other archaeological finds that can be seen, such as wall engravings. In the heart of this valley, near the base of some of the pyramids, there is a small museum called “Museo de Sitio Túcume”. This complex includes an amphitheater, an artisan gift shop, a juice bar, and paths leading to the various pyramids. Exploring the ancient dwelling place of the Sicán culture as you walk through the stunning Valley of the Pyramids is something that will take you back in time, and will leave you amazed at Peru’s rich and deeply-ingrained culture.

Activities

-El Pueblito de Los Ninos: a pathway that leads you through a small children’s pueblo, past rice fields and various animals.

- El Purgatorio Hill: climb to the top of this hill for a spectacular view of the whole city and its surrounding area. This hill is considered sacred by the locals, and is utilized by local “healing wizards” for weekly rituals.

- Huaca Las Balsas: an archaeological find associated with residential  living. A tent and raised walkway allow visitors to admire the beautiful motifs and carvings on the adobe walls. It provides another gift shop, bathrooms, and a small snack bar.
Interesting Facts

- Local shaman healers (or “curanderos”) utilize shamanic techniques and thehallucinogen San Pedro cactus in their weekly rituals on top of Purgatory Hill. Local people fear the site, causing Túcume to be one of the few ancient archaeological sites in the area to have never been hit by grave robbers.

- Túcume has been a hot spot for archaeologists, as 119 skeletons were found in one of the temples there. This seems like almost indisputable evidence of human sacrifice rituals, which may have been made in an attempts to stop the Spanish invasion of 1532.

- One of the huacas, Huaca Larga, was over 7 football fields long and is the largest adobe structure known of in the world.
