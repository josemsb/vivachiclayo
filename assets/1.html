The Bosque de Pómac is an equatorial dry forest that is one of only four locations in Peru classified as a historical sanctuary. It houses many different types of fauna and flora, though its main attraction is the Sicán ruins that are inside the sanctuary. The Sicán, also called the Lambayeque, were a group of people that inhabited the northern coast of Peru from about 750-1375 AD. The Bosque de Pómac houses 36 pyramids (called “huacas”) where archaeologists have discovered some of the finest Pre-Columbian pieces to date. These findings represent approximately 90% of all gold pieces found from the Lambayeque civilization. There are Lambayeque source populations that still call the Bosque de Pómac home, many of which are present near the site selling artisan crafts and cooking traditional meals. This site has been called an “unforgettable mix of nature and history”, as if you were brought back in time as a member of the Sicán culture. You will not want to pass up the opportunity to climb atop an ancient pyramid and see the dozens of ancient "huacas" rising up above the trees in this 23-square-mile forest. Be sure to bring bug spray to this area, the flies bite!

English/Spanish tours
They have an on-site Interpretation Center, where tour guides can be hired to show you the small museum area. You will want to tip them about 2 soles per person afterwards (or around 20 soles total). You may want to bring a taxi or other form of transportation along if you choose to visit the ruins, since many of the huacas are a considerable distance apart.
Time It Takes
Approximately 3 hours (plus transportation time)
Bathrooms?
Yes, the Interpretation Center has bathrooms and small bathrooms are available near some of the huacas.
Gift Shop?
No. However, they do have small snack stands located at the entrance of the complex.
Important Things to See:
The Millennial Tree
An Algorrobo (carob) tree that is said to be nearly a millennium old, and has many miracles and legends attributed to it by the local people.
Las Salinas Observatory
A viewpoint where you can see the vast forest, pyramids, and winding Leche River from above.
Huaca Las Ventanas
A truncated pyramid more than 32 feet high. Visitors can climb to the top!
Karl Weiss’ Beekeeping House
the old home of Karl Weiss, the first beekeeper in the area. It contains interactive activities relating to the production of honey.
Huaca Del Oro
A pyramid where a large portion of the gold was found. Tourists are permitted to hike up!
Interesting Fact: There have been 70 bird species, 7 mammal species, and 9 reptile species reported within the sanctuary. Two of the most noteworthy birds are the Cortarrama Peruvian Tumbes Swallow and the Rufous Flycatcher, both highly coveted by birdwatchers!
